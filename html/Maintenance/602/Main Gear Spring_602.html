Details of the main-gear spring are shown below in Fig Cross Reference. The main-gear spring is p/n 40086-4. It will fit on either side of the aircraft. It is machined of E-4340 aircraft quality steel, formed, heattreated and shot-peened for fatigue resistance. It is sandblasted and primed with an epoxy primer (US Paint R9006 Epoxy Primer) and finished with Alumigrip G-9046 yellow polyurethane paint.

The gear spring is clamped to the fuselage frame with a p/n 40087-1 clamp block, one NAS154A122 bolt and one NAS152DH51 bolt. MS20002C12 washers are used under the aft bolt head and MS20002C14 washers under the forward bolt head (make sure the chamfered side of the washer is under the bolt head). Various quantities of 3088A323 round shim washers are used between the clamp block and fuselage frame at the aft bolt location. The forward bolt uses p/n 3088A325 shims. 3088A shims are .010 thick. The shims are selected to allow the attaching bolts to be fully torqued without bending the clamp block. If too many thick shims are used, the gear connection will be loose and rattle during taxi or landing. If the gear leg becomes loose during service even though the clamp block bolts are at full torque, it will be necessary to re-shim the clamp block.

The inboard attachment of the spring to the fuselage frame uses a NAS156A92 bolt, MS20002C16 washer under the bolt head (be sure the chamfered side of the washer is next to the head), one or more MS20002-16 washers under the nut, AN310-16 nut and MS24665-360 cotter pin. The bolt is installed upside down and torque should be 6,400 inch-pounds (torque applied to the bolt head). If after torquing, you are unable to install the cotter pins, tighten until the next castellation aligns, but not to exceed an additional 60° of rotation.

It is necessary to hoist the aircraft as described under when changing the inboard bolt. Be sure to grease the bolt with general purpose grease before installing. Also, be sure the bolt is marked with a "R" on the head to indicate the threads were rolled after heat-treat.

The method employed at the factory to determine the number of required shims is to install the clamp block and fully torque the forward and rear outside bolts before installing the large inboard bolt. The block is checked for straightness with a small straight edge (a six-inch steel scale is OK) to be sure the block is not bent because of insufficient shims. The gear leg is then grasped at the lower end and given an outboard tug by hand. A moderate tug should move the inboard end of the leg down from the attach bushing in the fuselage frame a distance 1/8" to 1/4". If the distance is greater than 1/4" the connection is too loose, a thinner shim is tried and the process repeated. Once the proper shims are determined, the bolts are loosened and the large inboard bolt is installed. The clamp block bolts are then tightened to full torque and the clamp block bolts are then tightened to full torque (3,000 inch-pounds on aft bolt and 3,500 inch-pounds on forward bolt).

Since each main gear leg have a slightly different thickness and each clamp block is machined to a specific tolerance, it will be necessary to make a shim selection as described earlier if either the gear leg or the clamp block is changed.

When ordering new main gear springs or clamp blocks also order 4 each 3088A323 and 3088A325 shims in case they are needed. In addition, the large aluminum tapered shims at the axle attachment may need an addition or change, so it wise to order 2 ea. 40083-3 shims and 2 ea. 40083-2 shims in case of need. Whenever the airplane is ground-looped or involved in an incident which puts a permanent bend in one gear spring, the other spring must be changed due to potential overstress.

Obstruction Collision

If an obstruction is hit with the main gear, change the forward clamp bolt immediately and inspect the clamp block for straightness per . The forward clamp bolt may be changed without hoisting the aircraft. The parking brake should be set and care used to prevent disturbing the shims in place.
