Refer to Fig Cross Reference for the location of the fuel tank and header tank drains. These drains are designated for daily activation during the pilot's walk-around inspection as shown in the AIRPLANE FLIGHT MANUAL.

The header tank drain has a tendency to freeze up during fertilization operations if it is not activated daily. If this occurs, replace it immediately as it is the most important drain in the system. Be careful not to cross-thread drains when replacing.

The wing tanks have Curtis CCA-1650 drain valves and the header tank has a Curtis 39000 drain valve. If the valves leak, remove them and check the seals for nicks and look for trash between the seal and the valve seat. Apply 3H Permatex or an equivalent to the threads of all drains before they are reinstalled. Replace the valve if the seal is bad.

Valves with Enhanced Corrosion Protection

In 2025, Air Tractor received a report of a broken fuel shut off valve drain which allowed air to be introduced into the fuel system (Reference Service Letter #413). Subsequently, valves were certified to provide enhanced corrosion protection. Air Tractor suggests that you replace brass valves with stainless steel valves if your airplane is exposed to unusually corrosive environments and materials. See and .

Operating Precautions

Air Tractor received a report that the retaining ring at the end of the drain stem was cracked. If the retaining ring had cracked on the other side as well, the stem would have fallen out of the valve body, and all of the fuel would have been lost.

The cracked retaining ring was probably a result of allowing the stem to snap shut, with the force of the spring hammering shut the o-ring on the seat. It is suggested to ease the valve closed rather than allowing it to snap shut. Replace any valve found with a cracked retainer ring.
