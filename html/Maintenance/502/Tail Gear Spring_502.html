The tail gear spring is machined of E4340 aircraft quality steel, formed, heat-treated and shotpeened for fatigue resistance. It is sandblasted and primed with an epoxy primer and finished with polyurethane paint.

The forward attachment of the spring to the fuselage frame is with an AN bolt. It is clamped at the rear attachment with a clamp block which attaches to the fuselage frame with two AN bolts. There is an aluminum pad that is installed between the clamp block and the spring to allow the spring to work without putting secondary loads into the fuselage frame or attach bolts. There are square stainless shims and on occasion a spacer washer located between the clamp block and the fuselage frame. Per Section 3.1.9, during 12-month inspections the tail gear should be jacked at the stabilizer strut fittings as described under Section 2.0.4 and the tail spring checked for looseness at the clamp block. Looseness can be eliminated by removing one or more of the shims from under the clamp block. With the clamp block removed, also check the aluminum pad for condition.

Some early production aircraft used AN bolts in the clamp block for the tail spring. It is also possible that a tail spring safety cable is not installed. If your aircraft does not have NAS bolts in the clamp block or have the safety cable installed, it is recommended that the operator do so according to the information in Service Letter #169.

Besides saving a possible fatigue failure, the forward bolt tends to freeze up if not changed often. Be sure to grease the bolt and lightly torque the nut to XXX ft-lb. Do not overtighten as the spring must move on the bolt. Torque the clamp bolts to XXX inch-pounds (torque wrench on bolt head), and make sure there are sufficient shims between the clamp block and the fuselage frame to prevent bending the clamp block as the bolts are torqued.

The tail gear spring will fatigue and break at the same point in the life of the aircraft. The number of hours before failure will vary considerably as some operators make many more landings per flight hour than others, or carry heavier loads, or operate from very rough strips. Pilot technique is a factor also, since some pilots are very rough on airplanes. See Section 6 for the fatigue life of the tail gear spring.

Destruction of the old spring with a cutting torch is recommended.
