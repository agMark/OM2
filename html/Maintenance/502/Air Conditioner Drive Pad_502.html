The engine drive pad used to drive the air conditioner compressor is drive pad "C".

There have been three types of engine drive pads.

  • "unsealed" dry spline
  • "unsealed" wet spline
  • "sealed" wet spline

The term "dry" or "wet" refers to the spline inside the drive pad that the quill shaft engages.

The term "sealed" and "unsealed" refers to the pad being designed to accommodate rotating seals that are intended to keep engine oil inside the engine side of the drive pad.

INSERT Figure 2-114 Dry Spline

Dry splines can be identified by the plug which is installed inside the engine's shaft. Dry splines are designed to be packed with grease where shown in Figure 2-114.

Wet splines (inside the drive pad) are lubricated with engine oil. Visually, they can be identified by a small .040/.045 hole in the center of the engine's shaft which allows for a lubricating oil mist to escape the engine itself into the spline shaft and dive pad. (See Figure 2-115)

The "unsealed" drive pad configuration is disadvantageous because of its tendency to waste engine oil through the drive pad.

The engine build spec can be used to determine if the engine has or has been converted to a "sealed" type. For example, a PT6A-67F will have a "sealed" drive pad if the build spec is BS1309 or later.

There are two primary types of drive pad assemblies that bolt onto the engine's drive pad.

  • with unsealed bearings and oil bath
  • with unsealed bearings
    • quill shaft without o-rings for unsealed drive pad
    • quill shaft with o-rings for sealed drive pad

51628-1 is lubricated with engine oil and contains an internal bath. Bearing damage will occur if they are not properly lubricated with oil. This drive pad assembly may only be used on "unsealed" drive pads.
