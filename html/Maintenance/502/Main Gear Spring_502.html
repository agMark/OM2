Details of the main gear spring are shown below in Fig Cross Reference. The main gear spring will fit on either side of the aircraft. It is machined of E4340 aircraft quality steel, formed, heat-treated and shotpeened for fatigue resistance. It is sandblasted and primed with an epoxy primer and finished with polyurethane paint.

The gear spring is secured to the fuselage frame with a clamp block usng NAS bolts. Mil Spec washers go under the aft and forward bolt heads (making sure the chamfered side of the washer is under the bolt head). Various numbers of round shim washers go between the clamp block and fuselage frame at the aft and fwd bolts. The shims are selected to allow the bolts to be fully torqued without bending the clamp block. If too many thick shims are used the gear connection will be too loose and will rattle during taxi or landing. If the gear leg becomes loose during service (even though the clamp block bolts are at full torque) it will become necessary to re-shim the clamp block.

The inboard attachment of the spring to the fuselage frame is done with another NAS bolt, Mil Spec washer under the bolt head with the chamfered side of the washer is next to the head, one or more Mil Spec washers under the nut, an AN nut and a Mil Spec cotter pin. The bolt is installed upside down and torque should be XXXX inch-pounds (with the torque applied to the bolt head). If after torquing, you are unable to install the cotter pin, tighten the nut until the next castellation aligns, but not to exceed an additional 60° of rotation.

It is necessary to hoist the aircraft as described under when changing the inboard bolt. Be sure to grease the bolt shank with general purpose grease before installing. Also, be sure the bolt is marked with a "R" on the head to indicate the threads were rolled after heat treatment.

The method employed at the factory to determine the number of shims required is to install the clamp block and fully torque the bolts before installing the large inboard bolt. The block is checked for straightness with a small straight edge (a six-inch steel scale is sufficient) to be sure the block is not bent from a lack of sufficient shims. The gear leg should be grasped at the lower end while given an outboard tug by hand. A moderate tug should move the inboard end of the leg down from the attach bushing in the fuselage frame a distance 1/8" to 1/4". If the gap measured is larger than 1/4" the connection is too loose and a thinner shim should be attempted. The process should be repeated until the proper shims are determined. The clamp block bolts are then loosened and the large inboard bolt is installed. Then the clamp block bolts are retightened to the full torque required, XXXX inch-pounds on aft bolt and XXXX inch-pounds on the fwd bolt).

Since each main gear leg is slightly different in thickness, and since each clamp block is machined within a specific tolerance, it may be necessary to make a shim selection as described earlier, if either the gear leg or the clamp block is changed.

Whenever the airplane is ground-looped or involved in an incident that puts a permanent bend in one gear spring, the other spring must be changed as it may have been overstressed.
