Always wear protective clothing, rubber gloves and a face shield when servicing batteries. The battery status is checked by measuring the level of the electrolyte in each cell. The battery caps are first removed and then each cell is individually inspected. Using a light, if necessary, the electrolyte level should be observed at or slightly above the plastic split-ring visible through the filling hole.

If the electrolyte fluid level is low and there is a need to replenish fluid, the electrolyte level should be raised by adding only distilled water. The battery must not be overfilled or there is potential for electrolyte spewing out of the vent caps. The electrolyte level should never be more than one-quarter inch above the split ring.

When a battery seems weak or will not hold a charge after charging, replacement of the battery may be necessary. The health of individual cells is determined by checking the specific gravity of the electrolyte in each one.

The specific gravity check can be done with the use of a hydrometer constructed from acid-resistant materials. The specific gravity will range from 1.150 for a fully discharged cell to 1.300 for a fully charged cell. A specific ngravity of 1.260 or more is considered an adequate minimum charge. A specific gravity of less than 1.260 indicates charging is required. A variance of specific gravity more than 0.100 from other cells in the battery is an indication of a weak cell and the battery should be replaced. A specific gravity reading of 1.285 to 1.300 indicates a fully charged cell.

Commercial battery hydrometers are normally used for specific gravity checks. These hydrometers may display specific gravity readings or may have red and green bands. Depending on the state of charge and the ambient temperature, the specific gravity readings should not be interpreted as absolute but as comparative readings with other cells in the battery. A single cell whose specific gravity reading is at wide variances with other cells in the battery suggests a dead cell, requiring replacement of the battery. Consult the battery manufacturer's manual regarding how temperature may affect the measured specific gravity.

To check a G-246 (G-246AT) battery, first disconnect it from the other batteries and make sure that it is fully charged. Connect a 38-amp load to the battery and monitor the battery voltage. A 38-amp load can be obtained by connecting two 240-watt lamps in parallel. If the battery voltage is at least 21 volts or more after 24 minutes, the battery is serviceable. Monitor the electrolyte in the cells while the battery is under a load.

Boiling electrolyte in a cell while charging indicates a bad cell, though the appearance of occasional bubbles is indicative of the charging process itself. Replace the battery if the electrolyte in one or more cells boils. If the battery passes this test, it may be recharged and returned to service.
