The rudder and elevator both use Mil Spec bearings to attach to the vertical and horizontal stabilizers. These bearings will wear and out with time and will eventually require replacement.

With the rudder or elevator removed, the bearings should be checked for corrosion, free play and bolt looseness.

Freedom of movement should also be checked. The starting torque must not exceed 15 in-lbs. Experience shows that a bearing that can be rotated by hand using only finger pressure will not exceed 8 in-lbs. of starting torque.

Observe the staking pattern, which appear as indentations around the outer diameter of the bearing race when replacement of these bearings is required. The staking indentions must be removed in order to remove the bearing without creating further damage to the rib. Using a small rotary tool is recommended to remove a minimum amount of material. Once staking indentations are sufficiently removed, cautiously proceed to press the bearing out of the bore.

Once the bearing is removed, inspect the bore for gouges and damage. An oversized bore may require the use of an oversized repair bearing--contact Air Tractor Customer Service if this is the case.

If the bore is not damaged or oversized, proceed to install the new bearing as follows (from Process Specification 131):

  1. Verify that the housing bore is clean and free of burrs.
  2. Coat the bore surface with primer. Note: Zinc chromate primers have been used in the past and are still acceptable. Duplicolor DAP1690 Self-Etching Primer is also an approved primer in this role. Primer is sprayed into a cup and then brushed into the bore of the hole.
  3. While the primer paint is still wet press the bearing into the housing bore with an installation tool that keeps the bearing square to the housing bore and applies a uniformly distributed load around the perimeter of the bearing outer ring only. Note: The bearing housing (plate) must be supported flat and perpendicular to the axis of the bearing press motion close to the edge of the bore. Generally, an arbor press is used to press the bearing into the housing bore.
  4. Press the bearing into the bore to a depth such that the shoulders of the bearing inner ring are positioned equidistant from each side of the housing plate. Note: The bore fit should not require excessive force or the bearing may bind after installation. The bore must also not be so loose that the bearing can be inserted by hand.
  5. After bearing installation check the bearing for binding. This will be repeated again after the bearing is staked.
  6. Stake the bearing in place. Staking is a process of indenting the metal surrounding the bearing to impart radial pressure and mechanical interference with the chamfer of the bearing housing for retention. Use of an appropriate staking die set is highly recommended.

  7. Inspect staking impressions. They should not be large enough to cause binding but should secure the bearing axially.
  8. Check bearing axial and radial free play. Free play should be negligible (not measurable).
  9. Check starting or breakout torque is less than 15 in-lbs. If needed, it is acceptable to run the bearing for a few minutes before re-testing the starting torque.
