The master cylinders are manufactured by Cleveland. The lower end is bolted to aluminum straps connected to the rudder pedal. Both the strap and the pedal have bronze bushings that can be replaced when worn. The bushings are oil-impregnated and do not require lubrication. Details of the brake master cylinders are shown below in Fig Cross Reference.

The lower end of the master cylinder is attached to a Parker clevis with a Parker check nut. If the rudder pedal position (angle) should need changing for pilot comfort, the check nut can be loosened, and the clevis immediately below can be rotated with finger pressure in either direction to increase or decrease the angle that the brake pedal makes with the cockpit floor. When desired angle is obtained, torque the check nut to between 50-60 in-lb. To remove the master cylinder, loosen the check nut and rotate the plunger out of the clevis. Then remove the bolt attaching the master cylinder to the steel plates and disconnect the two brake lines at the cylinder.

To prevent the brake master cylinder clevis from disengaging from the master cylinder piston rod, it is recommended that the master cylinder piston rod clevis and check nut be inspected to ensure that the rod threads are properly engaged in the clevis internal threads and that the check nut is properly torqued. There is a thread engagement inspection hole in the side of the clevis. Insert a piece of safety wire in the hole to verify the piston rod is threaded into the clevis at least as far as the hole.

Finally, verify that the clevis and check nut on the master cylinder piston rod have been assembled with Loctite 242 (blue). If not yet accomplished, the assembly is to be done according to Drawing XXXXX and/or Service Letter #332.

If the bottom check nut is left in the original position, the pedal angle will be the same as before when the master cylinder is reinstalled. Be sure the cylinder is free to swing through the full range of rudder pedal travel. If it does not, the two plates which attach the cylinder are too close together and require an additional spacer/washer.

If a spot of brake fluid appears on the cockpit floor on the left-hand side it does not necessarily mean that the master cylinder is leaking--the usual cause is overfilling of the brake reservoir. In such cases, excess fluid returns to the reservoir during brake application and may leak out through the vent hole in the reservoir cap.
