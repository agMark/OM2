Each of the fuel tanks has two fuel quantity sending elements. These two sending elements are connected in series electrically. The outboard sending element is active in sensing fuel level when the fuel level is in the upper third of the tank. The inboard sending element is active when the fuel level is in the lower third of the tank. Both of the elements are active when the fuel level is in the mid-range. The inboard sender is insulated from ground (wing skin) by insulating washers that are used on both sides of the five holes in its flange.

The outboard sender is grounded to the wing skin through its mounting bolts.

The inboard and the outboard tank senders both use a Rochester resistive float probe. The sender is attached with five stainless screws, bushings and AN nuts. Both senders have Teflon washers under the bolt head for sealing purposes.

Troubleshooting

To verify functionality of the senders:

  1. Remove fuel tank covers as required to gain access to senders.
  2. Locate wiring exiting wing. This will be either a banana jack or a coax connector.
  3. Each sender should provide between 0 and 30 ohms of resistance. There are multiple ways to verify that each is functioning, the following assumes the tank has been drained and the senders are resting at the bottom of the tank.
    1. Measure resistance between signal wire (banana jack or center coax center conductor). Resistance should be very near 0 ohms with both probes fully down.
    2. Slowly raise one probe. Resistance will increase to approximately 30 ohms. Observe for erratic behavior any signs of a bad resistor wiper.
    3. With first probe all the way down, slowly exercise the other probe in the same way.
    4. Total resistance should be approximately 60 ohms when both probes are raised.
  4. If this procedure does not identify the cause of the issue, inspect for damaged wires or insulators on the inboard sender. Inadvertent grounding is a typical cause of issues.

To verify sender excitation from analog fuel gauges:

Excitation voltage is provided by the gauges. A small circuit board uses a Zener diode circuit to provide a small voltage that runs through the senders.

With infinite resistance (unplug the coax cable or banana jack), the voltage entering the wing should be approximately 0.5V. If otherwise, further inspect the gauge and trimming circuit.

Sender Replacement

The lefthand fuel tank sender has a shorter float arm than the righthand sender and is positioned differently in the tank. For these reasons the cork at the end of the float arm is still about 3” from the bottom of the fuel tank when the lefthand sender is at the end of its stroke and the receiver on the instrument panel shows “E”. This results in about 3 to 4 gallons of fuel still remaining in the lefthand tank when the receiver shows “E”. The fuel tanks are interconnected and if coordinated flight has been maintained, the righthand tank will show about 1/8 full at the same moment the lefthand tank shows empty. If the righthand sender is being replaced the cork at the end of the float arm should just touch the bottom of the tank when the arm is at the end of its stroke. It should touch the top of the tank at the upper end of the stroke. If it will not quite touch the tank bottom or touches too soon, bend the arm gently by hand to achieve the desired position. The lefthand sender will not touch the tank bottom, but will touch the tank top.

Before reinstalling the inspection plate on the fuel tank top, move the float arm through the full stroke with the battery switch ON to see that the sender is working properly. Seal the inspection plate per instruction in and re-install.
