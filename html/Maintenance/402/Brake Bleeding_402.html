If air enters the brake system because of worn O-rings or the replacement of brake system components, the brake pedal will become soft and the brakes lose some of their effectiveness. It will then be necessary to bleed the brakes to remove the air. Details of the brake-bleeding ports are shown below in Fig Cross Reference.

The conventional practice of placing a pressure pot line at the bleed screw location on the wheel cylinder and forcing fluid up through the system generally does not work. The factory practice is to place one person in the cockpit and another at the wheel cylinder with a bleed screw wrench and a clean bottle to catch the fluid. Before the bleeding process begins, it is necessary to obtain a short piece (approximately 6" long) of windshield wiper hose which will fit snugly over the bleed screw head so that all the fluid can be caught in the bottle and not wasted.

The procedure is as follows:

  1. The person in the cockpit pulls on the parking brake and leaves it on. The pedal being bled is then pumped several times until the pedal begins to get firm. The parking brake is left on since it is actually a one-way check valve.
  2. Then the person in the cockpit pushes on the pedal and shouts “open" to the person at the wheel cylinder. The person at the wheel cylinder slightly cracks open the bleed screw with a 1/4" open-end wrench. Brake fluid and air then rush through the bleed screw, hose and into the bottle.
  3. The person in the cockpit (who is holding pressure on the pedal) then shouts “close" as the pedal reaches the down position and the person on the ground immediately closes the bleed screw.
  4. The person in the cockpit repeats the procedure outlined in (1) to obtain a firm pedal and again repeats step (2) and (3).
  5. The above procedure is repeated as many as a dozen or so times until a firm brake is obtained with the parking brake off. Be sure to add brake fluid to the reservoir before the reservoir is emptied or else the entire procedure will need to be repeated. Don't immediately reuse the fluid in the bottle as it will be saturated with bubbles of air. However, when the bubbles have dissipated and the fluid is determined to still be clean, it may be reused.
  6. The brake bleeding process should be repeated for the other wheel.
